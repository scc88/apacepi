One product · two batch formulations

Batch-specific ePI variants

A marketed product has two batch formulations in circulation — a white tablet and a red tablet. Almost everything is identical, but they differ in the tablet's physical appearance, its film-coat colourant, and — because the red batch adds a colourant — a small safety difference too. Rather than duplicate a whole leaflet, we publish two ePI variants that share one common core and differ only where they genuinely differ.

◆ Shared across both variants

Indication, dosing, contraindications, warnings, side effects, packaging, storage, active ingredient and marketing authorisation — authored once.

◆ Specific to each batch

Tablet colour, appearance and imprint, the film-coat colourant excipient — and, because the red batch adds a colourant, a small safety difference (a colourant hypersensitivity note) plus the variant/batch identifier.

1 · What AMLOCARE is and what it is used for

Common

AMLOCARE contains amlodipine, which belongs to a group of medicines called calcium channel blockers. It is used to treat high blood pressure (hypertension) and a type of chest pain called angina.

2 · Before you take AMLOCARE

Common

Do not take AMLOCARE if you are allergic to amlodipine or other calcium channel blockers (dihydropyridines), or if you have very low blood pressure, shock, or a narrowing of the aortic heart valve. Tell your doctor if you have heart failure or liver problems.

3 · How to take AMLOCARE

Common

The usual dose is one 10 mg tablet once a day. Swallow the tablet with water, with or without food, at about the same time each day.

4 · Possible side effects

Batch-specific

Common to both variants: swollen ankles (oedema), headache, dizziness, flushing, palpitations. Tell your doctor or pharmacist if any side effect becomes troublesome.

How it's modelled in FHIR

Each variant is a separate ePI document Bundle — but the two Bundles reuse the same shared resources and differ only in the formulation layer. This is the same pattern as the SmPC ↔ PIL split, applied to formulation instead of audience.

◆ Shared resources — authored once, reused by both
  • Compositionthe leaflet spine (sections 1–4, 7) — indication, dosing, safety, pack
  • MedicinalProductDefinitionthe product identity
  • Ingredientactive substance (amlodipine) + core excipients
  • ClinicalUseDefinitionindication, contraindications, common side effects
  • PackagedProductDefinitionthe 30-tablet blister pack
  • Organizationmarketing authorisation holder
◆ Variant-specific — one small set per batch
  • ManufacturedItemDefinitionthe physical tablet — colour, shape, imprint
  • Ingredientfilm-coat colourant (titanium dioxide ± iron oxide red)
  • ClinicalUseDefinitioncolourant hypersensitivity note — red variant only
  • Composition §4–6appearance + variant-specific safety narrative
  • Bundle.identifiervariant / batch id (V1 white · V2 red)
The point: a batch difference touches only a handful of resources — and those differences aren't limited to appearance. Here the red batch's added colourant also carries a small safety note, so the variant layer covers formulation and its clinical consequences. The shared core is written and maintained once, so the two leaflets can never drift out of sync — and a patient scanning their specific pack always sees the leaflet that matches the tablet in their hand.
